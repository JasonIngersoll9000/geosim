Turns & Phases
The simulation runs in turns. Each turn represents roughly one week. Every turn has the same structure: you plan your actions, all actors act simultaneously, the engine resolves what happens, and you read the outcome.
Planning: You pick your actions
Resolution: The AI determines outcomes when all plans collide
Reaction: Actors can respond to surprising events immediately
Chronicle: A narrative summary of what happened
Escalation Ladder
Every actor sits on a ladder from 1 (peace) to 8 (nuclear war). Actors prefer the lowest level that achieves their goals. Moving up the ladder is costly — it risks retaliation, burns political capital, and is hard to reverse. Some actions hold your current rung, others escalate or de-escalate.
Rung 1-2: Diplomacy, sanctions
Rung 3-4: Covert operations, proxy warfare
Rung 5-6: Air campaigns, naval blockades
Rung 7: Ground invasion
Rung 8: Nuclear weapons
Fog of War
Actors make decisions based on what they believe, not what is true. Your intelligence about other countries may be wrong. If you think Iran's military is 80% destroyed but the real number is 50%, you might make overconfident decisions. This is how miscalculation works in real life.
Known unknowns: Things you know you don't know (e.g. "Where is the dispersed uranium?")
Unknown unknowns: Things you're wrong about but don't realize (only visible in Observer mode)
Constraint Cascades
Sometimes, conditions that prevent a dangerous action are removed one by one. When enough constraints fall, escalation that was previously impossible becomes rational. The system tracks these chains and alerts you when a cascade is forming.
Iran nuclear cascade:
1. Religious prohibition on nukes → REMOVED (Ayatollah killed)
2. Deterrence (fear of attack) → REMOVED (attack already happening)
3. International isolation risk → WEAKENED (already sanctioned)
Result: Nuclear breakout becomes rational from Iran's perspective
TurnPlan & Actions
Each turn, you pick a primary action plus optional supporting actions (up to 3). Actions have resource weights — you allocate your capacity between them. Compatible actions create synergies (bonuses). Incompatible actions create tensions (penalties).
Simple mode: Pick one recommended action and submit
Advanced mode: Build a multi-action plan with resource allocation
Example synergy: Air campaign + cyber operation = better targeting (+12%)
Example tension: Ceasefire talks + military escalation = credibility loss
Influence Channels
In real politics, policy doesn't simply follow public opinion. Different groups — lobbies, military establishments, media, the public — have different levels of influence over actual decisions. A lobby with 82/100 policy influence shapes decisions more than a public at 25/100, even if the public opposes the policy.
US example: The Israel lobby has 82/100 policy influence while the general public has 25/100. Public support is at 31%, but policy hasn't changed because the groups with actual influence still support the war.
Parameter Profiles
Every action can be executed in different ways. A ground operation could be a "surgical raid" (50K troops, low cost, limited objective) or "overwhelming force" (150K troops, high cost, full control). The same action produces completely different outcomes depending on which profile you choose.
Surgical Raid: Lower cost, lower risk, but achieves less
Overwhelming Force: Higher cost, higher risk, but more decisive
Covert: Cheapest, deniable, but requires good intelligence
Observer Mode vs Player Mode
In Player Mode, you control one country and make its decisions. The AI plays everyone else. In Observer Mode, the AI plays all actors. You watch, analyze, and can rewind to any point to try a different path. Observer mode shows you everything — including fog of war gaps and unknown unknowns.
