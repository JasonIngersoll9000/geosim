Strategic rationale

The primary justification for a ground operation is the Strait of Hormuz. Air and naval power alone cannot reopen it — Iran only needs a single drone to threaten any commercial vessel. A coastal occupation of the Iranian shoreline along the Strait is the only reliable way to guarantee safe passage. Secondary objective: establish a staging area for deeper operations if regime change remains the goal.

Prerequisites
This decision requires political authorization for ground combat operations, which the US currently lacks. It also requires sufficient ground forces in theater — current deployments are air/naval heavy. The US would need 80,000-150,000 troops, requiring 4-8 weeks of visible buildup.
Violates soft constraint: no ground invasion without political cover
Release conditions (homeland attack, mass casualty event) have not been met. Proceeding without a trigger event would dramatically accelerate the policy disconnect breaking point.
Intelligence gap warning
This decision requires knowledge of Iranian coastal defenses, mine placements, and anti-ship missile positions. Current confidence: LOW. The IRGC underground facility network is a known unknown.

Turn 2 — Week of March 8
Iran retaliates; the Strait closes
Iran's response was not the desperate lashing out that Washington had expected. It was methodical. Over five days, waves of Shahed-136 drones — cheap, expendable, numbering in the hundreds — swarmed toward Israeli air bases and US positions across the Gulf. Each wave forced the expenditure of Patriot interceptors costing orders of magnitude more than their targets. The math was simple and devastating: Iran was winning the accountant's war. Then came the strategic masterstroke. On March 11th, an IRGC commander announced that the Strait of Hormuz was closed to all non-allied shipping. Oil futures spiked 40% overnight. Russia quietly offered to fill the supply gap — at a premium.
