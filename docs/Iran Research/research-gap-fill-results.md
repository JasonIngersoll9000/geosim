### 2. GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrator (MOP)

## KEY ANALYTICAL NOTES FOR SIMULATION

1. **Interceptor-vs-missile arithmetic is the central constraint** of this conflict for the US/Israel side. Iran's cost-advantaged drones and missiles are depleting expensive interceptors faster than they can be replenished. The war is fundamentally a race between US/Israeli strike capacity to destroy Iranian launchers and Iran's ability to exhaust allied interceptor stocks.

2. **Radar destruction may matter more than interceptor counts**. Iran's systematic targeting of AN/TPY-2 and AN/FPS-132 radars degraded the BMD architecture's ability to detect and track threats — creating "detection failures" that look like interception failures.

3. **The munitions transition on Day 4** (Tomahawk → JDAM) is a critical inflection point. It reflects both tactical success (SEAD enabled stand-in operations) and strategic constraint (Tomahawk conservation for Indo-Pacific contingencies).

4. **Iranian drone resilience is systematically underestimated**. Shahed production requires no specialized infrastructure, and pre-war inventory estimates vary by 3x. The Pentagon privately acknowledged drones are a bigger problem than anticipated.

5. **Leadership decapitation was historically unprecedented** but has not produced regime collapse. The IRGC's architecture was designed to resist decapitation. Forces continue operating on standing orders.

6. **The Strait of Hormuz closure** has provided Iran more strategic leverage than its missile/drone campaign. ~2,000 ships stranded; oil above $100/barrel; global LNG disruption.

7. **Casualty figures for Iran are highly uncertain** and will remain so for months/years. Military casualties are almost certainly far higher than reported. Civilian casualties are contested between Iranian government, HR organizations, and US/Israeli claims of precision targeting.

Brent soared approximately 55% in March alone — a record monthly gain dating back to the contract's inception in 1988, surpassing the previous record of 46% set during the first Gulf War in September 1990. Brent reached a peak of $126/bbl. Prices pulled back when Trump signaled on ~March 25 that the U.S. military would wind down operations in "two or three weeks," briefly dipping below $100/bbl. Then on April 2, Trump's national address warning of further aggression sent WTI surging 11% in a single session to $111.54 and Brent to $109.03. Physical spot prices touched $141.36/bbl on April 2 before settling lower.

**Current (April 7):** Brent at ~$109.77/bbl; WTI at ~$112.41. Ceasefire discussions have introduced some downward pressure — Brent dipped to ~$108.76 on reports of a possible 45-day ceasefire — but Trump's reiterated threat to bomb every bridge and power plant in Iran by his Tuesday 8pm ET deadline pushed prices back up.

**Analyst projections:** Oxford Economics models a "prolonged war" scenario where Brent could surge to ~$190/bbl by August if the Strait remains closed through summer, though they're currently forecasting $113 average through Q2. Their base case projects that Hormuz stays closed until end of April. The Dallas Fed modeled a one-quarter closure yielding WTI averaging $98/bbl, with recovery to $68 if the Strait reopens after one quarter.

**Trajectory: VOLATILE / WORSENING** — ceasefire talks provide intermittent relief, but each escalation cycle (Kharg Island, B1 bridge, power grid threats) resets prices higher. The structural issue — Hormuz closure — remains unresolved.

## Strait of Hormuz Throughput

**Pre-war:** ~20 million bpd of crude and products transited daily, representing roughly 25–27% of global seaborne oil trade.

**Key dates:**

- **Feb 28:** War begins. IRGC issues warnings prohibiting vessel passage, leading to an effective halt in shipping.
- **~Mar 2:** IRGC official declares the Strait "closed," stating anyone attempting passage would have their ships set ablaze.
- **~Mar 4:** Iranian forces formally declare the Strait closed and begin carrying out attacks on ships. As of Mar 12, Iran had made 21 confirmed attacks on merchant vessels.
- **~Mar 10:** Iran begins laying mines in the Strait — initially only a few dozen, but retaining 80–90% of its small boats and minelayers capable of deploying hundreds more.
- **~Mar 11:** CENTCOM destroys 16 Iranian minelayers near the Strait.
- **Mar 19:** U.S. Armed Forces begin a military campaign to open the Strait.
- **~Mar 27:** IRGC announces the Strait is closed to any vessel going "to and from" ports of the U.S., Israel, and their allies — establishing a de facto "toll booth" system for others.
- **Late March–early April:** Transits tick up modestly under this selective system. 53 transits were recorded last week (the most since war began), up from 36 the prior week — still down over 90% from normal levels.
- **Apr 5:** Iran announces Iraqi ships can pass freely. A French container ship and Japanese-owned tanker made the first apparent transits linked to either country since the conflict began.

**Current status:** Export volumes of crude and refined products are at less than 10% of pre-conflict levels. Oxford Economics puts tanker passage down 98%. Nearly 15 million bpd of crude production plus another 4.5 million bpd of refined fuels are effectively stranded in the Gulf.

**Trajectory: MARGINALLY IMPROVING from total closure, but functionally still blocked.** The selective reopening allows a trickle, but commercially meaningful transit requires either a ceasefire or a forcible clearing operation that no coalition has yet undertaken.

## Iranian Oil Production

**Pre-war baseline:** Iran was producing ~3.3 million bpd of crude, exporting 1.3–1.6 million bpd (peaks exceeding 2 million bpd), with ~90% flowing through Kharg Island.

**Key dates:**

- **Feb 15–20:** Iran tripled its oil export rate pre-emptively and reduced storage to minimize risk of disruption.
- **Feb 28:** Strikes begin. Military, nuclear, and command infrastructure targeted — oil infrastructure initially spared.
- **~Mar 8:** U.S.-Israeli strikes hit several Iranian fuel sites including oil storage depots, signaling a new phase.
- **Mar 13–14:** U.S. Air Force conducts large-scale bombing of Kharg Island targeting 90+ military sites but deliberately sparing oil infrastructure. Iran's deputy governor of Bushehr claimed exports, imports, and island operations were proceeding normally afterward.
- **Post-Kharg raid:** Iran has exported 13.7 million barrels since the war started, with multiple tankers loading at Kharg. However, with Hormuz closed, these exports can only reach limited buyers.

**Current status:** Iran's oil infrastructure is largely physically intact — Kharg Island's oil terminal is undamaged, South Pars gas field remains largely operational. However, the combination of Hormuz closure and naval blockade means Iran faces a near-total and indefinite loss of export revenue, with an annual shortfall potentially exceeding $50 billion. Production is constrained by the inability to export, not by facility damage per se.

**Trajectory: WORSENING** — Trump's explicit threat to destroy oil wells, power plants, and Kharg Island if Hormuz isn't reopened by his Tuesday deadline makes physical destruction of oil infrastructure increasingly likely.

## Global Supply Gap

The IEA's Fatih Birol characterized the current loss at 12 million bpd — more than the 1973 and 1979 oil crises combined. This comprises:

- ~15 million bpd of crude stranded behind Hormuz
- Iranian exports (~1.5 million bpd) effectively zeroed
- Gulf state production cuts as onshore storage fills up (Iraq's production has fallen from 4.3 million bpd to 1.2 million bpd due to inability to export)
- Qatar's LNG production capacity reduced 17% after the March 18 strike on Ras Laffan

**Who's compensating:**

- Saudi Arabia cranked up its East-West Pipeline to full capacity of 7 million bpd by end of March, from an average of 770,000 bpd in January–February — this is the single largest compensating flow, routing oil to the Red Sea coast and bypassing Hormuz.
- OPEC+ agreed on April 5 to increase production by 206,000 bpd in May, though it's unclear how the oil reaches market with the Strait closed.
- TD Securities estimates nearly 1 billion barrels will be lost by end of April, comprising ~600 million barrels of crude and ~350 million barrels of refined products. Rapidan Energy projects a total net loss of 630 million barrels by end of June even accounting for redirected flows, emergency releases, and inventory drawdowns.

**Trajectory: WORSENING.** The IEA head warned that April will be "much worse than March" because March still benefited from oil cargoes that were already in transit before the war began.

## IEA Emergency Releases

On March 11, 2026, all 32 IEA member countries unanimously agreed to release 400 million barrels — the largest emergency release in the agency's history (more than double the 182.7 million released after Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine).

- The U.S. committed 172 million barrels from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, to be delivered over ~120 days.
- Asia-Oceania members began releasing stocks immediately; Americas and Europe stocks began flowing from end of March.

**Price impact:** Limited. U.S. crude briefly fell on the announcement but quickly climbed back above $90/bbl. As JPMorgan analysts noted, policy measures have limited impact unless safe passage through Hormuz is assured. Birol himself acknowledged that releases only "reduce the pain" and that the "cure" is reopening Hormuz.

The IEA is considering a second release but has not yet acted.

## Fertilizer Supply Disruption

The Persian Gulf accounts for roughly a third of global urea exports and a quarter of ammonia exports, with producers benefiting from cheap natural gas feedstock.

Urea prices have increased 50% since the start of the war as of late March. The LNG disruption also affects fertilizer production outside the Gulf. The price shock and fertilizer shortage during the spring planting season could reduce planting and yields of corn in the US — the main feedstock for beef, poultry, and dairy — and potentially increase global food prices into 2027.

Southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa are most exposed. The timeline to food security crisis is seasonal: the Northern Hemisphere spring planting window (March–May) is already underway, meaning reduced fertilizer application now will translate to lower yields at harvest (late summer/fall 2026) and elevated food prices through 2027.

**Trajectory: WORSENING and time-critical.** Each week of continued disruption during the planting season locks in lower agricultural yields.

## Global Recession Indicators

**Central bank actions:** The ECB postponed planned interest rate reductions on March 19, raised its 2026 inflation forecast, and cut GDP growth projections. The Bank of Japan is tightening policy. The Fed faces a bind between rising inflation and weakening growth.

**Market moves:** Global stocks fell 5.5% since the war began as of mid-March, with Asian markets worst hit. NYSE Composite down ~6%, Nasdaq down ~2.4%, Shanghai down ~1.86%. Global bond sell-off underway.

**Key forecasts:**

- Oxford Economics warns a prolonged conflict would slow world GDP growth to 1.4% in 2026 — constituting a global recession.
- EY-Parthenon's Gregory Daco raised the odds of a U.S. recession over the next year to 40% (vs. 15% baseline).
- UK inflation is expected to breach 5% in 2026. Energy-intensive EU/UK industries have imposed surcharges of up to 30%.
- Analysts project U.S. inflation could peak above 3%, euro-zone above 4%, Japan above 2.5%.

**Underlying vulnerability:** The U.S. economy was already weak — adding just 116,000 jobs in all of 2025, the lowest outside a recession since 2002, with job losses in five of the past nine months.

**Trajectory: WORSENING.** Every additional week of Hormuz closure compounds the damage. The structural concern is that physical infrastructure destruction (Qatar LNG, Gulf refineries) creates damage that persists long after any ceasefire — repairs to Qatar's LNG facilities alone could take years.

## Longer-Term Global Economic Implications

At current trajectory, the key downstream risks are:

1. **Stagflation entrenchment.** Oxford Economics warns that two significant inflation overshoots within four years could de-anchor inflation expectations, embedding higher risk premia into long-term rates and making the economy structurally more sensitive to future supply shocks.

2. **European deindustrialization.** Chemical and steel manufacturers have imposed surcharges of up to 30% to offset surging electricity and feedstock costs, potentially leading to permanent deindustrialization in some sectors — echoing the post-2022 Russia shock but more severe.

3. **Global South debt crisis.** Debt-ridden developing countries face a squeeze from higher fuel/food import costs and potential interest rate hikes in the Global North.

4. **Food security crisis.** Fertilizer shortages during the Northern Hemisphere planting season create a delayed but locked-in food price spike through 2027, with Africa and South/Southeast Asia most exposed.

5. **Energy transition acceleration.** The crisis is creating new opportunities for energy producers in the U.S., Canada, Brazil, and Guyana while accelerating policy interest in renewables and energy independence.

6. **GCC economic model collapse.** The war has caused a systemic collapse of the Gulf Cooperation Council economic model — Gulf states that built their economies on open maritime access and regional stability now face existential uncertainty.

# IRANIAN INFRASTRUCTURE

## Nuclear Facilities

**Natanz (uranium enrichment):**
- On ~March 2–3, IAEA confirmed damage to entrance buildings of the underground Fuel Enrichment Plant. Satellite imagery showed three buildings destroyed. The FEP had already been "severely damaged" during the June 2025 Twelve-Day War.
- The IAEA initially assessed no penetration of the underground enrichment halls, but power losses from strikes may have damaged centrifuges.
- The "Pickaxe" site under construction near Natanz does not appear to have been struck — it may be more deeply buried than Fordow.
- **Status:** Above-ground facilities destroyed (since June 2025); underground facilities degraded but extent unclear. No IAEA access since June 2025.

**Fordow (deeply buried enrichment):**
- Not targeted in the initial June 2025 Israeli strikes. The U.S. struck Fordow with 6 Massive Ordnance Penetrators on June 21, 2025, and Israel struck in the vicinity on June 23. The Israel Atomic Energy Commission assessed Fordow was rendered inoperable.
- In the current February 2026 campaign, Fordow was among the facilities targeted again. Combined June 2025 and February 2026 strikes assessed to have set Iran's program back "many years."
- **Status:** Assessed inoperable. No IAEA access.

**Bushehr (power reactor):**
- On March 3, Rosatom confirmed precautionary shutdown of Bushehr Unit 1 and suspended all construction work.
- Four projectile incidents have struck the Bushehr plant premises: February 28 (near the plant), March 17, March 24, and April 4.
- The April 4 strike killed one physical protection staff member and damaged a building from shockwave/fragments. No radiation increase reported.
- Rosatom has been conducting phased evacuations of Russian staff — the largest wave (198 people) departed April 4 toward the Armenian border.
- **Status:** Shut down precautionarily. Reactor not directly hit. Repeated near-misses. Active evacuation of personnel.

**Arak (heavy water reactor):** Under construction. IAEA reported no visible damage as of June 2025. No specific reporting of strikes in the current campaign.

**Parchin (military/testing):** Targeted in initial February 28 strikes as part of military infrastructure; specific current status not independently confirmed.

**Enriched uranium stockpile:** ~200 kg of Iran's 60% enriched uranium (enough for potentially 10+ warheads) may have been partially destroyed or compromised in the June 2025 and February 2026 strikes on Natanz and Fordow, but its exact disposition is unclear. Reports of a possible U.S. special forces operation to seize enriched uranium circulated in March.

---

## Oil/Gas Infrastructure

**Kharg Island (export terminal):**
- March 13: U.S. Air Force conducted a large bombing raid targeting 90+ military sites, sparing oil infrastructure. CENTCOM destroyed naval mine storage, missile bunkers, and other military sites.
- Oil terminal and export operations reported as continuing normally afterward. Satellite imagery showed tankers loading.
- However, analysts warn that Kharg remains under permanent threat — Trump explicitly stated oil infrastructure would be next if Hormuz remains closed.
- **Status:** Oil infrastructure intact. Military facilities destroyed. ~90% of Iran's export capacity passes through here but is functionally useless with Hormuz blocked.

**Bandar Abbas (refinery/port):**
- An Iranian warship was sunk by airstrike in the port of Bandar Abbas. Naval fuel facilities damaged.
- Damage to supporting naval fuel facilities at Bandar Abbas contributes to severe, long-duration export disruption.
- **Status:** Port operations severely degraded; refinery status unclear but impacted.

**South Pars gas field:**
- Israeli strikes hit South Pars — a major escalation that triggered Iranian retaliatory attacks on Gulf energy facilities.
- Natural gas production at South Pars remains "largely intact for now" but the cascading effects of strikes on surrounding infrastructure are degrading the broader system.
- **Status:** Partially operational, ~70–80% of pre-war. The field produces mostly for domestic consumption and power generation.

---

## Power Grid

- Late March (~March 29): Strikes hit the electricity grid in Alborz province, causing blackouts in parts of Tehran and Karaj. Power was partially restored but grid stability is degraded.
- Tehran experienced partial blackouts; the grid was already running a significant capacity deficit before the war.
- As of April 7, Trump has issued an explicit deadline — Tuesday 8pm ET — to begin destroying power plants and bridges if Iran doesn't reopen Hormuz. Analysts have identified that targeting 10–15 critical transmission substations (rather than individual plants) could cascade into a nationwide blackout lasting through summer 2027.
- **Status:** ~85–90% pre-war coverage estimated, with localized blackouts around Tehran/Karaj. Could deteriorate catastrophically if the April 8 deadline is enforced.

**Trajectory: CRITICAL inflection point at Trump's Tuesday deadline.**

## Water Treatment / Civilian Infrastructure

Limited specific reporting on water treatment collapse — information access is constrained by internet blackouts Iran imposed early in the conflict. Strikes have expanded to pharmaceutical/biological research facilities (Pasteur Institute, Darou Pakhsh), steel plants forced offline, and the B1 bridge. The combination of power grid degradation, industrial shutdown, and ongoing airstrikes is creating cascading civilian infrastructure pressure, though reporting from inside Iran is fragmented.

## Tehran-Karaj B1 Bridge

The B1 bridge connecting Tehran and Karaj was struck and destroyed on approximately April 2 (Wednesday night). It was under construction and described as the tallest bridge in the Middle East. U.S. officials indicated the bridge was a key missile supply route to western Iran, used to transfer missiles and components to launch sites. Trump posted a video of the collapse, stating it would "never be used again." Iran's IRGC threatened to hit major bridges in Gulf states in retaliation.

# GULF STATE INFRASTRUCTURE

## UAE — Habshan Gas Facility

ADNOC's Habshan complex is one of the world's largest gas processing facilities — five plants with total capacity of 6.1 billion standard cubic feet per day.

- **~March 19 (first incident):** Falling debris from a "successful" interception ignited a fire, forcing the facility to shut down. UAE condemned it as a "terrorist attack."
- **April 3 (second incident):** Debris from another interception caused two fires. One Egyptian national killed, four others injured. The facility suffered "significant damage" and assessment was ongoing.
- Since the start of the conflict, UAE air defenses have engaged a total of 438 ballistic missiles, 19 cruise missiles, and 2,012 UAVs.
- **Status:** Repeatedly shut down, currently assessing damage from April 3 strikes. Percentage operational unclear but significantly reduced.

## Saudi Aramco

- SAMREF refinery (Yanbu) — joint venture with Exxon — was targeted by a drone; minimal impact reported.
- Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline has been cranked to full capacity (7 million bpd) — a critical bypass route avoiding Hormuz entirely. This is the single most important compensating infrastructure in the entire crisis.
- Saudi air defenses have intercepted drones, cruise missiles over the Eastern Province throughout the conflict.
- No confirmed major damage to core Aramco production facilities (Abqaiq, Ras Tanura), though analysts warn these are high-value targets if escalation continues.
- Analysts warn that disruption at the Bab al-Mandeb chokepoint — where Houthis operate — could severely constrain the East-West Pipeline's Red Sea export route, potentially removing 4–5 million bpd from market.

## Other Gulf Energy Infrastructure Affected

- **Qatar — Ras Laffan LNG complex:** Struck on March 18, causing a 17% reduction in Qatar's LNG production capacity. Repairs estimated at 3–5 years. Fires contained by emergency teams. Global LNG supply reduced by 20%.
- **Kuwait — Mina Al-Ahmadi refinery:** Kuwait's largest refinery hit by drone attacks on April 3, setting units on fire. Kuwait Petroleum Corporation also reported significant damage to operational facilities from drone attacks on April 5. A desalination plant was also hit.
- **Bahrain — Bapco Oil Refinery:** Struck early in the conflict (~March 9). Bahrain reported 74 intercepted missiles and 92 drones since February 28.
- **UAE — Fujairah Oil Industry Zone:** Hit by drones causing fire early in the conflict. Shah oil field also struck by drones.
- **UAE — Dubai International Airport:** Damaged by drone strikes on the second day of the conflict, temporarily halting all flights.
- **UAE — AWS/Oracle data centers:** Amazon Web Services facilities in the UAE and Bahrain struck, causing significant damage. IRGC claimed it targeted an Oracle data center in Dubai on April 2.
- Iran's Bab al-Mandeb threat is now the next major escalation risk — an adviser to Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei warned that Iranian allies could shut this chokepoint as well, which would compound the crisis by cutting off the Saudi East-West Pipeline's Red Sea outlet.
