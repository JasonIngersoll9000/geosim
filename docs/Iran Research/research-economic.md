# Verified economic and strategic assessment of the US-Israel-Iran conflict

**Operation Epic Fury, launched February 28, 2026, has produced the most severe global energy disruption since the 1973 oil embargo.** Oil prices have surged roughly 50% in under three weeks, the Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed to Western-aligned shipping, and cascading effects are rippling through food, semiconductor, and financial markets worldwide. The conflict is reshaping geopolitical alignments in real time — benefiting Russia economically, testing China's strategic patience, and straining U.S. military capacity across multiple theaters. What follows is a verified, corrected, and quantified assessment organized by domain.

## 1. Oil markets: prices, volumes, and the Hormuz chokepoint

**Current prices and percentage increase**

✅ Oil prices have surged dramatically since the conflict began. **Brent crude stood at $108.78/bbl on March 18, 2026**, climbing to $109.95 intraday (+6.3% on the day alone). Pre-conflict Brent traded at **$65–73/bbl** (the EIA cites $71.32 on February 27; Al Jazeera pegs the baseline at ~$65; Capital Economics at ~$73).

📊 **The percentage increase from pre-conflict to current prices is approximately 47–68%**, depending on which baseline is used. The EIA's March 2026 Short-Term Energy Outlook states Brent settled at $94/bbl on March 9, "up about 50% from the beginning of the year." Prices briefly spiked to **$119–126/bbl** around March 9–10 before a temporary dip to ~$87 following the IEA emergency reserve release on March 11. WTI crude reached $95.70/bbl on March 12, up over 30% since the war began.

📊 **Forecasts remain elevated.** Goldman Sachs projects Brent above $100 for March. JP Morgan warned a full blockade could push prices to $120–130/bbl. Wood Mackenzie has said $150/bbl is "on the table" for a prolonged closure.

**Strait of Hormuz — closure status and transit volumes**

✅ **The 20% figure is accurate.** Approximately **20 million barrels per day** of crude oil and petroleum products transit the Strait of Hormuz, representing roughly one-fifth of global oil consumption. The IEA notes that in 2024, **34% of global crude oil trade** passed through the Strait. It also carries approximately **20% of global LNG trade**, primarily from Qatar.

✅ **Iran has effectively closed the Strait**, though no formal legal blockade has been declared. On March 2, IRGC commander Ebrahim Jabari announced the Strait "closed," warning that any vessels attempting to cross would be "set ablaze." Ship-tracking data initially showed a **70% reduction in traffic**; by March 14, commercial traffic had fallen to **zero AIS-confirmed crossings** — a first in modern history. Over **400 vessels** are holding position in the Gulf of Oman. Export volumes stand at **less than 10% of pre-conflict levels** (IEA). There have been **21 confirmed attacks on merchant ships** as of March 12.

✅ **Iran is selectively allowing passage.** On March 5, the IRGC announced the Strait would remain closed "only to ships from the US, Israel, and their Western allies." Turkey confirmed safe passage for a Turkish vessel on March 13. Two Indian-flagged gas carriers and a Saudi tanker carrying 1 million barrels bound for India were permitted through. Iran is in active talks with China about allowing crude and Qatari LNG carriers to transit. CNN reports Iran is considering allowing ships trading in **Chinese yuan** to pass — though this remains a proposal, not an operational arrangement.

➕ **Major marine war risk insurers (American Club, Gard, Skuld, NorthStandard, London P&I Club) have withdrawn coverage** for vessels in the Persian Gulf, creating a de facto insurance blockade even for routes Iran has not physically interdicted. Major shipping lines Maersk, MSC, Hapag-Lloyd, and CMA CGM have all suspended Hormuz transits.

**Infrastructure struck in Iran**

📊 Specific Iranian facilities damaged include: **Tehran oil depots** (Aghdasieh, Shahran, Shahr-e Rey) and the **Tehran refinery** (220,000 b/d capacity) struck on March 8; the **Karaj oil depot** in Alborz province on the same day; military targets on **Kharg Island** (which handles 90% of Iran's crude exports) struck March 13–14, with oil infrastructure deliberately preserved as a warning shot; and most significantly, Israel struck the **South Pars gas processing facility** (phases 3–6) on March 18, taking offline portions of the world's largest gas field.

**Gulf state infrastructure hit**

📊 The damage to Gulf state infrastructure has been extensive and spans multiple countries:

- **Qatar**: Iranian drone struck the **Ras Laffan LNG complex** on March 2, prompting QatarEnergy to halt all LNG production and declare force majeure on March 4. Ras Laffan produces **77 million tonnes per annum — 20% of global LNG**. The **Mesaieed Industrial City** was also struck.
- **Saudi Arabia**: The **Ras Tanura refinery** (550,000 bpd) suffered drone strikes and underwent precautionary shutdown.
- **Bahrain**: The **Sitra refinery** (380,000 bpd) was attacked; operator Bapco declared force majeure.
- **UAE**: The **Mussafah Fuel Terminal** in Abu Dhabi, **Fujairah Oil Terminal**, and a separate UAE gas field (set ablaze March 17) were all hit. Fujairah was struck again on March 14 after Iran accused the US of using UAE ports for the Kharg Island operation.
- **Oman**: Drones struck **Duqm port** and **Salalah port**.
- **Iraq**: Southern oilfield production fell **70%**, from 4.3 million to 1.3 million bpd.
- **Kuwait**: Kuwait Petroleum Corporation declared force majeure and reduced processing at all three refineries.

➕ On March 18, Iran's IRGC threatened to strike **five specific Gulf facilities within hours**: the SAMREF refinery and Al-Jubail petrochemical complex in Saudi Arabia, the Al-Hosn gas field in the UAE, and both the Mesaieed complex and Ras Laffan refinery in Qatar.

**Dubai housing and Gulf economic indicators**

⚠️ **The -30% figure requires important clarification.** The **Dubai Financial Market real estate stock index** fell approximately 30% in two weeks, from ~16,800 to ~11,700 points — wiping out all 2026 gains. However, **actual physical property prices have not crashed 30%.** Transaction data from the Dubai Land Department shows 3,570 sales worth AED 11.93 billion ($3.24B) between March 2–9, with analysts noting 3–7% negotiation windows rather than panic selling. The ultra-luxury segment continues recording sales. The -30% figure is accurate for the stock market index, not for physical real estate.

📊 Broader Gulf economic damage: Gulf oil producers lost an estimated **$15 billion** in the first two weeks. Tourism losses run approximately **$600 million per day** region-wide. Drone debris struck near **Dubai International Airport** (limited flights resumed), the **Burj Al Arab hotel**, and **Palm Jumeirah**. The DFM closed for two trading sessions — nearly unprecedented. UAE corporate bonds became the worst performers in emerging markets.

**Alternative supply sources**

📊 Three bypass pipelines exist but can only partially offset the disruption. Saudi Arabia's **East-West Pipeline (Petroline)** was converted to full capacity on March 11 (5–7 million bpd maximum; Yanbu loadings rose from 1.1M to 2.2M bpd). The UAE's **ADCOP pipeline** has ~440,000 bpd spare capacity at 71% utilization, though the Fujairah terminal was itself struck. Iran's **Goreh-Jask pipeline** has been minimally used. **Total bypass capacity is 3.5–5.5 million bpd** against a **~20 million bpd gap** — leaving 15–17 million bpd unaddressed. Critically, bypass infrastructure handles crude oil only; no bypass exists for refined products, and approximately 30% of Europe's diesel imports and 50% of jet fuel came from the Middle East pre-conflict.

📊 The **IEA authorized a 400-million-barrel emergency reserve release on March 11 — the largest in IEA history**, more than double the 182.7M barrels released after Russia invaded Ukraine. The US is contributing 172 million barrels from the SPR (which held ~415 million barrels pre-release). However, 400M barrels equals only **~20 days of normal Hormuz flows** or about **4 days of global consumption**. Prices briefly dipped to $87 then quickly rebounded. OPEC+ announced a 206,000 bpd production increase starting April, but most spare capacity (estimated 3.5–4.1 million bpd) is in Saudi Arabia and the UAE — countries whose exports are constrained by the Hormuz closure itself.

**European energy impacts**

📊 European gas prices (TTF benchmark) surged from **€31.9/MWh on February 28 to €54.3/MWh on March 4 — a 70%+ jump** — reaching a three-year high of **€63.77/MWh** before easing below €50 after the IEA release. EU gas storage has fallen **below 30%**, a five-year low. The EU started 2026 with only 46 bcm in storage versus 60 bcm in 2025 and 77 bcm in 2024. Capital Economics projects that if the war continues for months, **eurozone GDP growth slows to 0.5% year-on-year and inflation peaks above 4%**. The ECB has acknowledged that the "probability of a rate hike has increased." Approximately 30% of Europe's diesel and 50% of jet fuel imports came from the Middle East. The Atlantic Council concluded that "even if the conflict were to end today, Europe is likely already heading toward an energy crisis."

**Asian energy impacts**

📊 **84% of oil and 83% of LNG shipped through Hormuz was bound for Asia.** Asian LNG spot prices more than doubled to **$25.40/MMBtu**. Japan — which imports **95% of its oil from the Middle East, ~70% via Hormuz** — announced its largest-ever release from national reserves (~80 million barrels). South Korea's KOSPI suffered its **biggest crash since 2008**, dropping up to 12% on March 4 and triggering circuit breakers; Seoul imposed its first fuel price cap in nearly three decades. India, importing 90% of crude with **less than 30 days** of strategic reserves, invoked emergency powers to redirect LPG from industrial to household use. The Philippines saw diesel prices jump **38.6%** and imposed four-day government workweeks. Bangladesh closed universities, imposed fuel rationing, and stationed troops at fuel depots. Pakistan ordered a four-day government workweek and saw fuel prices rise 20% in one week — the largest increase in its history.

## 2. The petrodollar question: separating reality from rhetoric

**Yuan-for-oil at Hormuz**

⚠️ **There is no confirmed operational yuan-for-oil arrangement at the Strait of Hormuz.** CNN reported around March 14 that a senior Iranian official confirmed Iran is **"considering"** allowing tankers to pass if cargo is traded in yuan. This remains a proposal under consideration. Chinese analysts reacted "cautiously," citing operational feasibility limits, security risks, and potential strain on China-US relations (SCMP, March 14). The Gulf Research Center's chairman called the immediate practical impact "limited" but symbolically significant.

✅ What is confirmed: Iran continues exporting oil through the Strait — CNN reports "almost the same volumes as before the war" — with satellite images showing 2.7 million barrels loading at Kharg Island on a single day. **Nearly all Iranian exports go to China, almost certainly settled in yuan.** European Business Magazine claims 11.7–16.5 million barrels of Iranian crude have transited to China via shadow fleet under IRGC protection since February 28, though this claim relies on less-established sourcing.

**Pre-existing yuan oil trade baseline**

📊 The yuan's role in oil markets was growing before this conflict but remained marginal. China's Shanghai International Energy Exchange yuan-denominated crude futures launched in March 2018 and accounted for **~10.5% of global crude futures volume** by 2020. Russia-China trade shifted rapidly after the 2022 Ukraine sanctions — **$19.14 billion** in crude oil was settled in yuan in 2025. Over 20% of China's oil imports from Russia were settled in yuan by mid-2023. Saudi Arabia has expressed openness to discussing yuan-denominated deals but has made **no meaningful operational shift**, constrained by its dollar peg and limited yuan recycling channels. A **$7 billion currency swap** exists between China and Saudi Arabia.

📊 **The US dollar still facilitates approximately 80–90% of global oil transactions.** The yuan's share of global oil payments remained **below 5%** as of mid-2025, primarily confined to bilateral deals with Russia, Iran, and Venezuela.

**BRICS de-dollarization: concrete vs. aspirational**

⚠️ BRICS has built real payment infrastructure but remains far from challenging dollar dominance. Concrete steps include **BRICS Pay** (a cross-border messaging system linking Russia's SPFS, China's CIPS, and India's UPI), the **mBridge** digital currency platform (which processed over $55.5 billion in transactions by November 2025, with digital yuan accounting for 95%), and the **New Development Bank** increasing local-currency lending to roughly one-third of loans. BRICS-10 now represents ~46% of global population and ~37% of world GDP.

However, **no single BRICS currency exists or is imminent.** India's External Affairs Minister Jaishankar explicitly stated in March 2025: "I do not believe we have any policy to have a replacement to the dollar." Putin acknowledged a single currency "would take time." India refuses to settle Russian oil payments in yuan, reflecting a fundamental internal divide. The dollar's share of global reserves has declined gradually — from **72% in 2001 to ~57% in 2025** — but this erosion is spread across many smaller currencies rather than toward any single challenger. **The yuan comprises only ~2% of global reserves** versus the dollar's 57% and the euro's 20%.

**Impact on US borrowing costs**

📊 Economists estimate the petrodollar "exorbitant privilege" is worth roughly **$225–270 billion annually** to the American economy (Globe and Mail, March 2026). The CBO estimates the average long-run interest rate increases by **2 basis points for every 1 percentage-point increase in debt as a percentage of GDP**. Most economists see gradual evolution toward a multipolar currency system rather than sudden collapse — consensus holds that dollar replacement is likely decades away, though higher borrowing costs from erosion are plausible.

**US national debt**

✅ **The ~$39 trillion figure is essentially accurate.** Total gross national debt stood at **$38.86 trillion** as of March 4, 2026, growing at $7.23 billion per day. The Joint Economic Committee projects the US will reach $39 trillion by approximately March 25.

📊 **Annual interest payments have nearly tripled since 2020**, reaching approximately **$970 billion in FY2025**. The CBO projects net interest costs of **$1.0 trillion in FY2026**, rising to $2.1 trillion by 2036. Interest costs now exceed both national defense and Medicaid spending. The average interest rate on total marketable debt has risen to **3.355%** from 1.512% five years ago — a 122% increase. Critically, **33% of publicly held marketable debt matures within 12 months**, meaning higher rates flow through quickly as debt rolls over. Current bid-to-cover ratios remain above 2.0, indicating robust Treasury demand — for now.

**Historical precedent**

➕ **The 1956 Suez Crisis is the strongest historical parallel.** Britain invaded Egypt to control the Suez Canal; the US threatened to sell sterling bonds, and the Bank of England lost $200+ million in reserves in November 1956 alone (total reserves were only ~$2 billion). Britain was forced into humiliating withdrawal, and the crisis accelerated sterling's decline as a reserve currency through the 1960s–70s. The parallel: a major power conducting military operations at a critical energy chokepoint while heavily indebted and dependent on foreign financing. Research by Eichengreen and Flandreau (2009) shows the dollar overtook sterling as early as the mid-1920s — only about a decade after WWI — suggesting reserve currency transitions can happen faster than the conventional 30–70 year narrative implies.

## 3. Russia emerges as the conflict's clearest short-term beneficiary

**Intelligence sharing — confirmed**

✅ **Russia is actively providing intelligence to Iran to target US forces.** This was confirmed independently on March 6, 2026, by the Washington Post (three US officials), CNN (multiple sources), NBC News (four sources), and AP (two officials). Russia is sharing **satellite imagery and targeting information** including "locations and movements of American troops, ships, and aircraft." The Kanopus-V satellite (re-designated "Khayyam" for Iranian operations) provides round-the-clock optical and radar imagery. By March 17, the Wall Street Journal reported Russia had **expanded** intelligence sharing to include improved drone guidance enabling "more precise strikes on radar and missile systems in the Gulf."

⚠️ **China's intelligence role is less certain.** US intelligence suggests China "may be preparing" to provide financial assistance, spare parts, and missile components (CNN, March 6), but China is described as "more cautious" — it wants the war to end because it endangers Chinese energy supplies.

**US sanctions relief on Russia — confirmed**

✅ **The Trump administration issued a 30-day sanctions waiver on Russian oil on March 13, 2026** (Treasury General License GL 134). The waiver authorized delivery and sale of Russian crude oil and petroleum products loaded on vessels as of March 12, valid through April 11. It covers approximately **120–128 million barrels** of Russian oil on water across ~30 global locations. The waiver also temporarily paused the US price cap on Russian oil, though EU/UK sanctions remain in place. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent called it "narrowly tailored" to "stabilize global energy markets."

📊 The European reaction was sharply negative. European Council President António Costa called it "very concerning" for European security. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz said "we believe it is wrong." French President Macron said the Hormuz situation "in no way" justifies lifting sanctions. Ukraine's Zelensky warned: "Lifting sanctions will lead to a strengthening of Russia's position. It spends the money from energy sales on weapons."

**Russia's oil revenue windfall**

📊 Before the conflict, Russia's oil exports were at their weakest since the 2022 invasion — **$9.5 billion** in February 2026 with Urals crude trading at ~$40/barrel, roughly $30 below Brent. Post-conflict, Urals has climbed above **$70/barrel** — well above Russia's $59 budget assumption. In some cases, Russian crude is being sold to India at a **premium** to Brent, reversing earlier discounts. Carnegie Russia Eurasia Center analyst Sergey Vakulenko estimates each $10/month increase generates approximately **$1.63 billion in additional state revenue**. Russia is netting approximately **$150 million per day** from higher-priced oil. If prices average $90/barrel for the year, Russia could see **~$55 billion in additional revenue**. Indian imports of Russian crude reached ~1.5 million bpd in mid-March, up ~50% from early February.

**Impact on the Ukraine war**

📊 The most immediate impact is an air defense crisis. Zelensky stated that **800+ Patriot missiles were used in the Middle East in just three days** — more than Ukraine has received throughout the entire war since 2022. The US produces only **~60–65 Patriot missiles per month**. European officials acknowledged the Iran war will "complicate their commitment" to provide Ukraine with such weaponry. The US asked Ukraine to contribute drone interceptor technology to defend American bases in the Middle East — Ukraine deployed interceptors and specialists to protect US bases in Jordan. Max Bergmann of CSIS noted: "The US use of Tomahawk missiles [in Iran] likely means whatever chance of the Trump administration providing that platform to Ukraine is gone."

❌ **Russia has NOT made net territorial gains since the Iran conflict began — the opposite has occurred.** Between February 10 and March 10, Russian forces actually **lost 57 square miles**, in notable contrast to gaining 182 square miles in the prior four weeks. Ukraine recorded its **first territorial gains since 2023**, recapturing 257–460 square kilometers in Dnipropetrovsk and Zaporizhia regions. Reports indicate elite Russian airborne units being redeployed from offensive sectors to counter Ukrainian counterattacks. This may reflect Russian manpower exhaustion rather than any direct conflict effect, though the diversion of Western attention clearly benefits Russia's broader strategic position.

**Russia-Iran military relationship**

📊 Pre-war confirmed arms transfers include contracts for **48 Su-35 fighter jets** (delivery 2026–2028), **Yak-130 training jets** (at least one squadron delivered), **Mi-28 attack helicopters** (up to 6 delivered by January 2026), and a **€500 million deal for 500 Verba MANPADS** signed December 2025. During the active conflict, Russia is providing improved Shahed drone modifications — developed through years of Russian combat-testing in Ukraine — including better targeting, handling, and flight profiles. However, no large-scale wartime arms transfers have been confirmed. Both countries consume the same weapons categories (air defense, ballistic missiles, drones), and Russia's defense industrial capacity is strained by Ukraine.

## 4. China plays a waiting game on Taiwan

**US military redeployment — confirmed and specific**

✅ **The US has executed its largest Middle East military buildup since the 2003 Iraq invasion**, drawing specific assets from the Indo-Pacific. Confirmed redeployments include: the **USS Tripoli Amphibious Ready Group** (homeported in Sasebo, Japan) with the **31st Marine Expeditionary Unit** from Okinawa, spotted transiting the South China Sea at high speed on March 16; **THAAD missile defense components from South Korea's Seongju base**, confirmed by both the Washington Post and South Korean President Lee Jae-myung; **Patriot missile interceptors** being loaded onto C-17 transports at Osan Air Force Base for Middle East delivery; and **1,000 guided munitions withdrawn from Korea** before the war began. Two carrier strike groups (USS Abraham Lincoln and USS Gerald R. Ford) plus **120+ aircraft** including F-22s deployed to Israel's Ovda Airbase are in theater.

📊 The munitions depletion is staggering. An estimated **300+ Patriot and other interceptors** were used in the first 36 hours. The US burned through **14% of its total THAAD interceptor stockpile** against Iran. The first six days of Operation Epic Fury cost an estimated **$11.3 billion**, with $5.6 billion in munitions consumed in the first two days alone.

➕ **The THAAD removal from South Korea is particularly significant** beyond its direct military value — the AN/TPY-2 radars associated with THAAD can peer approximately 3,000 kilometers into Chinese territory. Their removal represents a major intelligence benefit for China regardless of any Taiwan action.

**Current US posture in Asia**

⚠️ The situation is contested among analysts. The Pentagon states it is "managing global responsibilities" while ensuring forces "remain capable of deterring aggression in the Indo-Pacific." Undersecretary Elbridge Colby said his first call after the Iran attack was to the Philippine defense minister. The USS George Washington carrier remains in Japan. F-15EX deployments to Kadena Air Base are proceeding. US-Philippines exercises (500+ planned for 2026) remain scheduled. However, current and former defense officials in Asia are "growing concerned that more American firepower will be shifted over time if the war drags on" (Bloomberg). Even after the conflict, depleted munitions stockpiles "could take years to replace, leaving Taiwan and other places vulnerable."

**China has NOT exploited the Taiwan opening**

❌ **Contrary to what might be expected, China has de-escalated around Taiwan since the conflict began.** Beginning February 27, Taiwan recorded **13 consecutive days without Chinese warplanes flying near the island** — described as "frankly unlike anything we've seen in recent history" by PLATracker founder Ben Lewis. PLA ADIZ incursions dropped below 200/month in January–February 2026, down from 300+/month average. Flights resumed March 12–15 but at moderate levels. ISW/AEI assessed: "China has given little indication it's looking to ramp up tensions with Taiwan, calling for an urgent ceasefire in the Middle East while dialing back pressure on the island's air defenses." Analysts attribute this to China's desire to preserve the planned Trump-Xi summit and avoid giving the US a pretext for redirecting attention.

However, **China continues long-term preparations.** Coast guard incursions near Kinmen and Pratas Island continued in February. The December 2025 "Justice Mission" drills rehearsed a full maritime blockade of Taiwan covering a larger area than any exercise since 2022. China's 2026 government work report changed "oppose Taiwan independence" to the stronger "**crack down on** Taiwan independence." Defense spending increased **7% to ~$278 billion** despite GDP growth targets falling to their lowest since 1991.

**China's official position and Iranian oil imports**

✅ China's official position condemns the US-Israeli strikes as lacking UN authorization and violating international law. Foreign Minister Wang Yi called the killing of Khamenei "unacceptable." China is diplomatically active — Special Envoy Zhai Jun conducted a regional tour starting March 8 — but is pressuring Iran on the Strait of Hormuz to protect Qatari gas exports. China refused Trump's demand to police the Strait.

📊 China imported **1.38 million barrels per day** of Iranian oil in 2025, absorbing 80–90% of Iran's total exports at a **$8–10/barrel discount**. Pre-war stockpiling was aggressive: Iran exported 2.16 mbd in February 2026 (highest since July 2018), all to China. During the conflict, China continues receiving approximately **1.25 million bpd**, with Chinese-flagged vessels granted passage while others are blocked. China holds an estimated **1.2–1.4 billion barrels** in strategic reserves — roughly 120 days of net imports — plus 40 million barrels in floating storage.

**Chinese military equipment to Iran**

⚠️ **This is the most contested area.** Middle East Eye reported (February 27, citing three officials) that China delivered "offensive" weapons including loitering munitions and air defense systems **before** the strikes began. Reuters reported Iran was "nearing completion" of a deal for Chinese **CM-302 supersonic anti-ship missiles** — described as a "complete game-changer" — but delivery status is unclear. China's MFA dismissed the CM-302 report as "not true." What is confirmed: China granted Iran full military access to its **BeiDou-3 encrypted satellite navigation system** in 2021, which experts believe Iran is currently using to guide attacks. Two Iranian ships departed China carrying **sodium perchlorate** (solid rocket fuel precursor) the week of March 2. ISW/AEI assessed China is "unlikely to provide material support to Iran." The most credible picture suggests pre-war transfers of limited systems, ongoing negotiations for larger deals, but no confirmed large-scale wartime resupply.

## 5. Broader economic damage extends far beyond energy

**Financial markets**

📊 Global stocks have fallen **5.5%** since the war began — the worst monthly performance since 2022 (Bloomberg). The S&P 500 is down approximately 4.2%; the Nasdaq down 2.4%. South Korea's KOSPI suffered its **biggest crash since 2008**, dropping up to 12% on March 4 and triggering circuit breakers. Energy stocks rose 3.23% while financial services fell 3.37%. The 10-year Treasury yield rose from **3.96% to ~4.25%** (+29 basis points) and the 30-year yield climbed to ~4.90% — both stocks and bonds sold off simultaneously as inflation fears overwhelmed safe-haven demand. Fed rate cut expectations have been **pushed back to mid-2027**.

**Food prices and the fertilizer shock**

➕ **This may be the most underappreciated cascading risk.** Approximately one-third of globally traded urea passes through the Strait of Hormuz. Gulf countries account for **49% of global urea exports and ~30% of ammonia exports**. They also supply **45% of global sulfur**. US fertilizer prices jumped from **$516/metric ton to $683/metric ton** — a 32% spike — with roughly 25% of US farmers having not yet purchased fertilizer for the spring planting season. Analysts project US corn acreage could shrink by **1–1.5 million acres**. Wheat prices are near two-year peaks. Palm oil surged as much as 10%, with soybean oil heading for its longest run of gains since 2008. Sub-Saharan Africa, where fertilizer use is already minimal, is particularly vulnerable to reduced global harvests.

**Countries facing acute crisis**

📊 Several nations face genuine emergencies. **Bangladesh** has closed universities, imposed fuel rationing, stationed troops at fuel depots, and seen power cuts double to 5 hours daily — its garment sector (world's second-largest exporter) is threatened. **Pakistan** ordered a four-day government workweek, closed schools, and saw the largest fuel price increase in its history (20% in one week). **India** invoked emergency powers to redirect LPG; approximately 20% of Mumbai's hotels and restaurants have shut down or scaled back. **The Philippines** imposed four-day government workweeks after diesel rose 38.6%. **Vietnam** has less than 20 days of oil reserves. **Egypt's** President el-Sisi declared the economy in a "state of near-emergency." The Asian Development Bank singled out the Philippines, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka as facing the strongest macroeconomic effects.

**US consumer economy**

📊 The national average gasoline price reached **$3.79/gallon** as of March 17 — up **$0.87 (+30%)** from $2.92 one month prior. Diesel hit **$5.04/gallon**, up $1.34. California gas reached $5.34–5.48/gallon. The University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment Index fell to **57.9 in March**, with the survey director noting that post-February 28 readings "completely erased initial gains." Q4 2025 GDP was revised down to **0.7% annualized** (from initially reported 1.4%). February payrolls showed employment falling by **92,000** with unemployment rising to 4.4%. Higher gas prices function as a regressive tax disproportionately hurting lower-income households — analysts describe an emerging "K-shaped" economic divergence.

➕ **The semiconductor supply chain faces a hidden threat.** Qatar's Ras Laffan facility — offline since March 2 — produces approximately one-third of the world's helium supply, critical for chip manufacturing. South Korea sources **65% of its helium** from Qatar and produces roughly two-thirds of global memory chips. Additionally, two-thirds of global bromine production comes from Israel and Jordan (used in circuit fabrication); South Korea sources 90% of its bromine from Israel. Experts estimate a minimum 2–3 month helium supply disruption, with 4–6 months before the supply chain normalizes. A Morningstar analyst warned that an extended war could "materially delay AI infrastructure builds."

**Inflation and GDP outlook**

📊 For every sustained $10/barrel oil price increase, economists estimate approximately **+0.3 percentage points of headline inflation and -0.1 to -0.2 points of GDP growth**. At current prices (~$100+ versus ~$70 pre-war), that implies roughly +1.25 points of additional inflation and -0.5 to -0.75 points of GDP drag for the US. Capital Economics projects that in a prolonged war scenario, **eurozone inflation peaks above 4%, US inflation above 3%, Japanese inflation above 2.5%**. GDP forecasts: US growth of ~2.25% (outperforming); eurozone slowing to 0.5% with probable Q2 contraction; China falling below 3%. The IMF's Georgieva warned of significant inflationary risk on March 9. The specter of **stagflation** — echoing 1973 and 1979 — is now widely discussed.

## Strategic summary for simulation foundations

The US-Israel-Iran conflict has created a **multi-domain economic shock** that is more structurally significant than any energy disruption since the 1973 embargo. Five dynamics define the strategic landscape:

**First, the Hormuz closure is a partial rather than total blockade** — Iran is weaponizing access to create a two-tier system favoring China and neutral states, producing a fragmented rather than uniformly disrupted oil market. This selectivity gives the yuan-for-oil proposal its symbolic power, even though the dollar still dominates **80–90% of global oil trade** and the yuan represents just 2% of global reserves.

**Second, Russia is the conflict's clearest economic beneficiary**, gaining an oil revenue windfall (Urals crude rising from ~$40 to above $70), a historic sanctions waiver, and diversion of Western air defense from Ukraine — all without committing combat forces. The intelligence-sharing arrangement gives Russia influence without exposure.

**Third, China is playing for the long term.** It has not exploited the Taiwan window, prioritizing summit diplomacy and energy security over military adventurism. Its pre-war stockpiling, continued Iranian oil imports via shadow fleet, and advanced renewable transition leave it better positioned than most to absorb the shock. The removal of THAAD radars from South Korea is a strategic intelligence windfall regardless.

**Fourth, the cascading effects on food, semiconductors, and developing economies may prove more destabilizing than the oil shock itself.** The fertilizer disruption threatens Northern Hemisphere spring planting; the helium shortage threatens chip manufacturing; and nations like Bangladesh, Pakistan, and the Philippines face genuine economic emergencies that could produce political instability.

**Fifth, the US fiscal position amplifies risk.** At **$38.9 trillion in debt with ~$1 trillion in annual interest payments**, the US is spending approximately $50 billion per week on borrowing and $11.3 billion on six days of military operations. Treasury yields have risen 29 basis points since the conflict began. The historical parallel to Suez — where overextension at a critical chokepoint accelerated reserve currency erosion — is imprecise but instructive. The relevant question is not whether the dollar gets replaced, but what happens to financial assumptions if its 25-year decline from 72% to 57% of global reserves accelerates by even a few percentage points.
