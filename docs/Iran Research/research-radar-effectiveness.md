### 1.1 AN/TPY-2 — Muwaffaq Salti Air Base, Jordan

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **System** | AN/TPY-2 X-band AESA (Raytheon), terminal mode |
| **Location** | Muwaffaq Salti AB, Jordan (~32.0°N, 36.8°E) |

| **Supports** | THAAD battery (primary fire control); feeds Patriot PAC-3 via JTIDS/Link-16 |
| **Status** | **DESTROYED** — confirmed via satellite imagery (Planet Labs, March 2, 2026). Two 13-foot craters adjacent to radar. All five trailer-mounted components appear destroyed or severely damaged. Struck by Iranian loitering munitions/ballistic missiles during Feb 28–Mar 2 strikes. |
| **Replacement cost** | ~$500 million (MDA budget documents) |
| **Replacement timeline** | Months minimum — requires redeployment from another theater. THAAD components being moved from South Korea (confirmed). |

**Effectiveness Impact:**
- **Coverage contribution:** CRITICAL (>30%) — This was the primary upper-tier ballistic missile defense sensor covering the Jordan–Israel–northern Saudi corridor. It provided terminal-mode fire control for the Jordan-based THAAD battery and contributed tracking data to the integrated BMD picture.
- **Sector covered:** Eastern Mediterranean approaches; Jordan–Israel corridor; western Iraq overwatch; inbound Iranian MRBMs/IRBMs transiting toward Israel/Jordan.
- **Degradation estimate:** Without this radar, the Jordan THAAD battery cannot independently acquire or track targets. It can only fire interceptors using external cueing from other sensors (Aegis ships, Israeli radars), which reduces engagement timeline by an estimated 30–50% and increases interceptors-per-missile required by roughly 50–100% to maintain equivalent P(kill) for that sector. Alternatively, if interceptor allocation stays fixed, leaker rate in this corridor increases an estimated 15–30%.
- **Confidence:** HIGH — destruction confirmed by multiple independent imagery analyses (CNN, Planet Labs, ABC News, Armament Research Services).

### 1.5 AN/FPS-132 Block 5 — Umm Dahal, Qatar

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **System** | AN/FPS-132 Block 5 solid-state phased array early warning radar (3-face, 360° coverage) |
| **Location** | Umm Dahal, near Al Udeid AB, Qatar (~25.1°N, 51.3°E) |

| **Supports** | Strategic early warning for entire theater; feeds THAAD, Patriot, Aegis BMD, and Israeli Arrow systems with early track data |
| **Status** | **DAMAGED** — Confirmed hit on northern face of radar installation (the face oriented toward Iran). **Effectiveness Impact:**
- **Coverage contribution:** CRITICAL (>30%) — This was the theater's only fixed strategic early warning radar. It is the functional equivalent of the BMEWS stations in Alaska, Greenland, and the UK — but for the Middle East theater.
- **Sector covered:** 360° coverage but primary value was the northern face covering Iranian launch sites. Without this warning, defensive systems must engage later in the missile trajectory (terminal phase only), reducing engagement opportunities from 2–3 shots to potentially 1 shot. This effectively doubles or triples the interceptor requirement per missile across the entire theater, or increases leaker rates by an estimated 20–40% theater-wide.
- **Confidence:** HIGH — Damage confirmed by satellite imagery and Qatar. Degree of functional impairment to the northern face is assessed but not precisely verified.

## SECTION 3: IRANIAN AIR DEFENSE RADARS

**Context note:** Iranian air defenses were among the first targets struck in both the June 2025 Twelve-Day War and the February 28, 2026 opening strikes. Israel claimed to have destroyed ~120 air defense systems (~one-third of Iran's pre-war total) by mid-2025. The February 2026 strikes further targeted surviving systems. However, Iran has demonstrated some residual capability — two US F-15s were shot down on April 4, 2026, contradicting Trump's claim of "total air dominance." Pre-war, Iran attempted to reconstitute by integrating surviving S-300 components with indigenous Bavar-373 systems and repositioning launchers around Tehran and Isfahan (confirmed by February 2026 satellite imagery).

### Key Simulation Implications

**1. The "sensor gap" is now more consequential than the "interceptor gap."**

The systematic targeting of AN/TPY-2 radars, the AN/FPS-132, and SATCOM infrastructure represents a coherent anti-access/area-denial strategy targeting the "eyes" rather than the "fists" of the defense network. For simulation modeling: Iran's attack on ~10 radar sites created disproportionate degradation relative to the number of munitions expended. Each $20,000–$60,000 drone that destroyed a $500M–$1.1B radar achieved a cost-exchange ratio of roughly 10,000:1 to 55,000:1.

**3. Aegis ships are now the critical remaining sensor/shooter asset.**
With ground-based radars degraded, naval BMD platforms bear a disproportionate burden. This makes them both more valuable and more vulnerable — Iran's anti-ship missile arsenal (Khalij Fars, Hormuz-2, mines) now has enhanced strategic significance because destroying an Aegis ship removes both a sensor AND a shooter.

**4. Iran's air defenses are degraded but not eliminated.**
The April 4, 2026 F-15 shootdowns demonstrate residual capability. For simulation: model Iranian IADS as ~15–25% effective compared to pre-war baseline, concentrated around Tehran and Isfahan, with large areas of Iranian airspace effectively undefended at medium-high altitude but retaining some low-altitude SHORAD/MANPADS threat.
